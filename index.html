The
Protocol

You are not your mind. You are the one training it. The mind proposes; you decide what drives. Every card below is one rep of that decision. Reset each morning. No logs, no rearview — the proof lives in the doing.

0/8 reps

Morning

First 60 min

You're not starting the day. You're setting the algorithm's operating parameters before the world sets them for you.

Body before mind

Real physical exertion — strength, sprints, hard conditioning. Not a stretch. Actual training, before anything else.

Discharges the stress chemistry that would leak into every decision today, and gives you your first proof-of-execution before the world offers an excuse. A body that has done hard things doesn't negotiate with easy resistance.

No phone, first hour

Attention touches your agenda first — not notifications, not feeds, not other people's urgency.

The first thing your attention lands on trains what your brain treats as urgent for the rest of the day. Feed it your priorities or it will run on everyone else's.

Decide today's stance

Say your standard for the day out loud, once, with conviction. Not an affirmation — a decision. Keep the same line for at least a week.

The person who wins today decided who they were before the day had a chance to tell them who to be.

Through the day

All day · live reps

This is where the real work happens. Mindset doesn't live in the morning ritual — it lives in the seconds after a setback.

Catch the story

Every time a setback fires an automatic thought — "I'm not good enough," "I always mess this up" — run the sequence:

1Notice the thought arose.
2Label it: "That's the old pattern talking, not a fact."
3Ask: "What does this moment actually require of me right now?"

You can't stop the thought from arising. You can stop it from being the last word. Mental toughness isn't immunity to setbacks — it's recovery speed. Count every catch below; each one is a rep.

catches

Match task to energy

Hardest cognitive or physical work goes in your peak window (usually within 4–6 hours of waking). Admin and low-stakes work goes in the dip.

Willpower runs out when you fight your own biology all day. Working with it is why some people seem to have endless drive.

Training

Session

Training isn't just physical adaptation. It's controlled adversity on demand — the forge for everything above.

The mental rep

In the hardest moment of the session — when your body proposes quitting before it has to — don't distract from the discomfort. Stay present with it and choose the next rep anyway.

That specific choice, repeated hundreds of times across months, is what builds mental toughness. The neural machinery for "push through discomfort toward a chosen outcome" doesn't know the difference between a barbell and a business problem.

Night

Last 30 min

This is where the architecture consolidates. Sleep is when today's patterns get written into long-term structure.

Close the loop

Whatever happened today — good or bad — gets one short acknowledgment: "That happened. It's done. Tomorrow starts clean." Then it doesn't get to run wild at 11pm.

Ruminating right before sleep is saving corrupted data into long-term memory. Close the file first.

Protect the sleep

Fixed wake time, every day — it matters more than a fixed bedtime. Guard the last 30 minutes: dim, quiet, no feeds.

The least glamorous, most decisive lever in the entire system. No one sustains elite output on erratic sleep — the research isn't close.

Version discipline · every 4–6 weeks Ask one question: "Where did I flinch this month — and is this practice still addressing it, or am I going through motions?" If it's gone stale, increase the load: harder training, longer discomfort tolerance, bigger stakes in what you rehearse. The mind adapts to whatever load you give it. A system that never increases load stops training anything.
