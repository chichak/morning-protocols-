Reading the tape:
Order Flow & Volume Profile

Price charts show you where the market has been. Order flow shows you who is trading right now, and volume profile shows you where the real business was done. This manual explains both disciplines from the ground up, fuses them into a repeatable trade workflow, and compares the platforms professionals actually use.

01 / Foundation

The market is a two-way auction

Both tools come from the same idea, so start here. Every futures market is a continuous double auction: buyers and sellers negotiate until they find prices where business gets done.

The auction has exactly two jobs: advertise price to attract activity, and facilitate trade at prices both sides accept. From this, the two states every session cycles through:

Balance (acceptance)

Buyers and sellers agree on value. Price rotates around a fair area, volume builds a fat, bell-shaped distribution, and mean-reversion trades work. Roughly 70–80% of intraday time is spent here.

Imbalance (rejection / discovery)

One side becomes aggressive, price leaves the agreed area quickly on elongated bars and thin volume, and it keeps moving until it finds new sellers or buyers. Momentum and breakout trades work here — fades get run over.

Volume profile is the map of this auction — it shows where value was built and where price was rejected. Order flow is the live microphone — it lets you hear which side is aggressive at this exact moment. The entire methodology in this manual is: use the map to pick locations, use the microphone to time triggers. Neither tool alone is a strategy; the edge is in the combination and in your discipline executing it.

02 / The microphone

Order flow: who is trading right now

Order flow analysis reads executed trades and resting liquidity at the tick level. It answers one question price alone cannot: are the people trading right now aggressive buyers, aggressive sellers, or passive absorbers?

Aggressors vs. passives — the core distinction

Every trade needs two parties: a passive trader resting a limit order in the book, and an aggressor crossing the spread with a market order to hit it. A trade printed at the ask means an aggressive buyer lifted a passive seller. A trade at the bid means an aggressive seller hit a passive buyer. Aggressors move price; passives stop it. Every order-flow tool is a different way of counting and visualizing this split.

The footprint chart, cell by cell

A footprint (cluster / numbers-bar) chart explodes each candle into rows — one per price — showing SELL volume × BUY volume traded there. Read it diagonally: buyers lifting the ask at one price are competing against sellers hitting the bid one tick below. When one side out-trades the other by a large multiple (commonly 300–400%+), that cell is an imbalance.

The five signals that matter

  • Stacked imbalances. Three or more consecutive diagonal imbalances on one side. Marks initiative activity; the zone frequently defends the first retest. Failure of a stacked-imbalance zone is itself a signal — the side that committed there is trapped.
  • Absorption. Heavy aggressive volume prints into a level but price does not move — passive limit orders are eating every market order. High volume + no progress = someone big is standing there. Fading the exhausted aggressor after confirmed absorption is one of the highest-quality reversal reads in this discipline.
  • Exhaustion. Volume dries to near zero at the extreme of a move (single prints, tiny cells). The aggressive side ran out of participants. Often pairs with absorption at the same turn.
  • Delta divergence. Price makes a new extreme but cumulative delta doesn't (or vice versa). Effort without result: aggressors are pushing, passives are winning.
  • Trapped traders. A burst of aggressive buying at a breakout that immediately fails leaves buyers underwater above the market. Their forced exits fuel the move back down — look for the delta flip that confirms they're bailing.

The DOM: reading resting liquidity

The Depth-of-Market ladder shows passive limit orders waiting at each price. Executed trades tell you what happened; the DOM hints at what large players intend — with the caveat that resting orders can be pulled (spoofed), so weight executed volume over displayed size.

03 / The map

Volume profile: where the business was done

A volume profile turns the y-axis into a histogram: total volume traded at each price, regardless of when. It reveals which prices the market accepted as fair — and which it rejected.

The reference levels, defined

LevelDefinitionHow it trades
POCPrice with the most traded volume in the profileMagnet inside balance; strong reaction level on first touch after time away
Value Area (VA)Zone containing ~70% of total volume around the POCInside VA → rotation rules. Outside VA with acceptance → trend rules
VAH / VALUpper / lower boundary of the value areaThe decision line: rejection = fade back to POC; acceptance = range extension
HVNLocal volume peak (there can be several)Price stalls, chops, and mean-reverts; poor breakout locations, good target zones
LVNLocal volume trough between two HVNsPrice rejects or accelerates through; excellent stop placement and breakout retest zones
Naked / virgin POCA prior session's POC never revisited sinceLong-memory magnet; first retouch usually gets a reaction worth trading
Composite profileProfile built over many sessions (week, month, whole range)Defines the macro balance; its edges and LVNs frame swing-level context

Profile shapes tell you what kind of day it was

Acceptance vs. rejection — the only judgment that matters at a level

Rejection: price probes beyond a reference (VAH, LVN, prior high), volume stays thin, and it snaps back within minutes. Trade back toward value.

Acceptance: price spends time beyond the reference, builds volume there (the profile starts fattening), and value migrates. Stop fading — the market is repricing. A useful heuristic: two consecutive 30-minute periods building volume beyond the level = acceptance.

04 / The framework

The fusion: location → trigger → confirmation

Most losing order-flow traders read footprints in the middle of nowhere. Most losing profile traders fade levels blindly. The professional workflow chains the tools so each does the one job it's good at.

What the profile is for

  • Marking tomorrow's battle lines tonight: POC, VAH, VAL, naked POCs, LVNs, composite edges
  • Classifying the day type (balance vs. imbalance) so you run the right playbook
  • Placing targets at HVNs/POCs and stops behind LVNs

What order flow is for

  • Timing the entry at a level instead of guessing with a resting limit
  • Detecting who's trapped and which side just gave up
  • Telling you within 1–2 ticks that you're wrong — the cheapest stop-out you'll ever get
05 / The playbook

Three complete trade setups

Each setup is written as a full specification: context, location, order-flow trigger, entry, stop, targets, and — most important — the exact condition that proves you wrong. Trade them long or short by mirroring the logic. Master one before adding the next.

SETUP A

Value-area fade — the "80% rule" rotation

MEAN REVERSIONBALANCE DAYS
Context
Market opens outside yesterday's value area, then trades back inside and holds there. Classic auction stat: once price re-enters and is accepted (roughly two 30-min periods inside), it traverses to the other side of the value area a large majority of the time — the "80% rule."
Location
Long version: re-entry above yesterday's VAL after an open below value. Short version: re-entry below VAH after an open above value.
Order-flow trigger
On the pullback that retests VAL from above: sell aggression dries up (shrinking sell cells, single prints), a bar prints absorption (heavy bid-side volume, price refuses to break VAL), then a delta flip with buy imbalances lifting off the level.
Entry
Buy the close of the trigger bar or the first 1–2 tick pullback into the stacked-imbalance zone.
Stop
Below the absorption low / below VAL by 2–4 ticks (instrument-dependent). If VAL is reclaimed by sellers with acceptance, the premise is dead — no averaging down.
Targets
T1: yesterday's POC (scale ⅓–½, move stop to entry). T2: yesterday's VAH — the full 80%-rule traverse. Trail behind bar-POC clusters.
Abort / invalidation
Two consecutive periods building volume back below VAL; CVD makes new session lows; the big passive bid you leaned on gets pulled or eaten without reloading.
SETUP B

LVN break & retest — trend continuation

WITH TRENDIMBALANCE DAYS
Context
Price is leaving a balance area with conviction — elongated bars, rising volume — and drives through an LVN that separates the old balance from open space (or from the next distribution). You do not chase the break; you trade the retest.
Location
The LVN itself, plus the edge of the balance just broken (old VAH now acting as support for longs). The thinner the LVN, the cleaner the level.
Order-flow trigger
On the pullback into the LVN: sell volume is light (nobody defends a low-volume zone), the footprint shows a quick absorption of the remaining sellers, then stacked buy imbalances print off the zone. The breakout bar's own stacked imbalances should hold as support — if they fail, buyers from the break are trapped.
Entry
Buy in the LVN as the delta flips positive, or on the break of the trigger bar's high.
Stop
Back inside the prior balance, beyond the old VAH/HVN — the point where the breakout premise is factually wrong. LVNs make natural stop homes because acceptance back through them changes the story.
Targets
T1: the next HVN / naked POC above (profiles are target maps). T2: measured move — the height of the broken balance projected from the break.
Abort / invalidation
Retest volume is heavy and price stalls in the LVN (that's acceptance, not rejection — the break is failing); CVD diverges against the trend at the retest; price re-enters the old value area and holds.
SETUP C

Absorption reversal at a key reference

COUNTER-TRENDA+ PATIENCE TRADE
Context
An extended move drives into a major, pre-marked reference: prior day high/low, overnight high/low, naked POC, or composite-profile edge. Late momentum traders are piling in exactly where informed passive players want to transact the other way.
Location
The reference level only. This setup does not exist in the middle of a range — that is its entire protection.
Order-flow trigger
The three-part reversal signature, in order: (1) Absorption — huge aggressive buy volume prints at the level but price stops dead (an iceberg on the DOM keeps reloading). (2) Exhaustion — the next push shows tiny cells and single prints at a marginal new high. (3) Flip — sell imbalances stack, delta turns sharply negative, and CVD diverges from the marginal high.
Entry
Short as the flip bar closes, or on the first weak retest of the absorption zone from below.
Stop
Above the absorption high plus 2–4 ticks. This is one of the tightest structural stops in trading — the whole edge is that the level either holds or it doesn't.
Targets
T1: nearest HVN / session POC below. T2: value area on the other side of the move. Trapped-buyer liquidation often accelerates the first leg — take T1 quickly and let the rest run risk-free.
Abort / invalidation
The iceberg is pulled and price accepts above the reference (absorption failed — that's fuel for continuation, and a signal to consider the opposite trade); no exhaustion appears and buy imbalances keep stacking through the level.

What kills these setups in real accounts

  • Trading the trigger without the location. Imbalances print constantly; they only mean something at a level.
  • Anticipating the flip. Divergence and absorption are conditions. You enter on the flip, not before it.
  • Moving the stop. Each spec includes the sentence that proves you wrong. When it happens, you're out — order flow's gift is telling you early.
  • Trading every day the same way. Setup A is for balance days, Setup B for imbalance days. Running the wrong playbook for the day type is the most common structural error.
06 / The routine

The daily workflow, minute by minute

The setups only work inside a process. This is the routine, written for a US index-futures session (adapt the clock to your market).

  1. T−60 MIN · PREPARATION

    Build the map

    Check the economic calendar (CPI, FOMC, NFP — no fresh positions into tier-1 releases). Review the overnight session: range, volume, where value built relative to yesterday.

    Mark, in order of priority: yesterday's POC / VAH / VAL, any naked POCs within reach, overnight high/low, the governing composite profile's HVNs and LVNs, and yesterday's profile shape.

  2. T−15 MIN · HYPOTHESES

    Write two scenarios, not one prediction

    "Scenario A: we open inside value → balance rules, fade edges toward POC (Setup A logic)." "Scenario B: we open/drive outside value and get acceptance → imbalance rules, buy LVN retests (Setup B)." Decide in advance which levels flip you from A to B. You are preparing reactions, not forecasts.

  3. 09:30–09:45 · THE OPEN

    Classify, don't trade

    Watch the opening type: open-drive (one-directional aggression from the bell — strongest conviction), open-test-drive (probe one way, reject, drive the other), or open-auction (rotation — balance likely). Unless a textbook A+ trigger prints at a pre-marked level, the first 15 minutes are for information, not entries.

  4. 09:45–11:30 · EXECUTION WINDOW

    Hunt only at your levels

    Price away from a marked level = hands off the mouse. Price at a level = run the entry checklist below. This window has the volume and the two-sided participation the setups need.

    • Is price at a level I marked before the session? Which setup does this match?
    • Does the day type agree? (No Setup A fades on an open-drive trend day.)
    • Order-flow trigger printed and completed — absorption/exhaustion/flip, not just divergence?
    • Stop location defined by structure, and position sized so that stop = my fixed risk unit?
    • First target ≥ 1.5R away with a real HVN/POC behind it?
    • Am I calm? (If you're revenge-trading, the answer is no trade.)
  5. 11:30–13:30 · MIDDAY

    Stand down

    Volume thins, ranges compress, order-flow signals turn noisy and untrustworthy. Most consistent intraday traders simply don't trade lunch. Update your levels; review the morning's executions.

  6. 13:30–16:00 · AFTERNOON

    Trade the afternoon auction selectively

    Re-engagement often comes after 14:00 (and around 14:00 on Fed days). The same rules apply, with extra respect for the developing day's own VAH/VAL/POC, which by now are mature references.

  7. POST-CLOSE · REVIEW

    Journal the process, not the P&L

    Screenshot every trade with the footprint at entry. Grade each on: location quality, trigger quality, management. A losing trade executed perfectly is a good trade; a winner taken off-plan is a bad one. This loop — not any indicator — is what compounds skill.

07 / Survival

Risk rules & the honest math

Nothing in this manual "maximizes wins" by itself. What a disciplined workflow actually does is filter out low-quality trades and cap the cost of the losers — that's the only lever you fully control.

Hard rules

  • Risk per trade: a fixed unit, commonly 0.25–1% of account. Size the position to the structural stop — never widen the stop to fit a size.
  • Daily stop: 2 losing trades or −2R, whichever comes first → flat, done, review.
  • Weekly circuit breaker: −5R → reduce size by half the following week.
  • One setup at a time until it's profitable over 40+ logged trades in sim/replay before real size.

Expectancy beats win rate

A 45% win rate with 2R average winners is a solidly profitable system:

E = (0.45 × 2R) − (0.55 × 1R) = +0.35R / trade

The setups above are engineered for exactly this shape: tight structural stops at levels, targets at the next HVN/POC. Chasing a high win rate with wide stops inverts the math.

Read this before risking money

Day trading futures and other leveraged products carries a high risk of loss, and the majority of retail day traders lose money over time. Order flow and volume profile are professional-grade analytical frameworks, not guarantees — the same tools are used by traders who fail. Past patterns do not assure future results.

This document is educational material, not financial advice, and it isn't tailored to your situation. Trade in simulation until you have a logged, positive-expectancy sample; start live with the smallest size available (micro contracts exist for this reason); and never trade money you cannot afford to lose.
